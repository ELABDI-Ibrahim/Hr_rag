Domain context: Compliance due diligence in insurance (AXA) — ordinal 3-class verdict, asymmetric error costs, regulatory explainability mandate, two fundamentally different evidence paths (document vs declarative), human expert as ground truth.

Error asymmetry rule: A missed non-compliant (false positive on compliant) is categorically more costly than a false alarm. This changes how you weight κ, set confidence thresholds, and prioritise recall over precision on the non-compliant class.

Verdict / classification agreement
Weighted Cohen's kappa
What: Agreement between AI verdict and human expert, weighted by ordinal distance.
Why it matters here: This is THE primary metric for this domain. Ordinal penalties (0/1/2) mean a compliant↔non_compliant error is penalised 2× harder than a compliant↔partial error. This matches the real-world cost structure exactly.
Interpretation: κ>0.8 excellent, 0.6–0.8 good, <0.4 insufficient for production use.
Implementation: Done. utils/evaluation.py with the exact 3×3 penalty matrix.
Research backing: Weighted κ is the standard for ordinal clinical/compliance classification tasks — used in ICD coding, audit grading, and risk rating.

Confidence Calibration
What: Are the model's confidence scores actually predictive of accuracy? If the AI says 0.9 confidence, it should be correct 90% of the time.
Why it matters here: SSI experts use the confidence score to decide whether to deep-review a case. Overconfident wrong verdicts are dangerous — an expert will skip manual review on a 0.92-confidence "compliant" that is actually non-compliant.
To implement: Plot confidence vs accuracy in buckets (0–0.5, 0.5–0.7, 0.7–0.9, 0.9–1.0). Compute ECE (Expected Calibration Error).
Calibration fix: If AI is systematically overconfident, add a post-hoc calibration step or add calibration instruction to the verdict prompt.
